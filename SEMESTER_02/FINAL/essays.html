Pride And Prejudice Essay

Hahn, Kenny
Kokkinis, p.5
1/21/17
Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen is a classical novel that comments on early 1800s
relationship norms, and the role of women in society. It involves a deep, complicated plot centered
around love and marriage, and is full of interesting, colorful characters. Characters that are, even
though they are timeless, are not completely unique. Most of the characters in this book are
classical archetypes, such as the Tragic Hero, the Villain, Shrew, and Free Spirit, their names
being Mr. Darcy, Mr. Wickham, Elizabeth Bennett, and her little sister, Lydia bennet.

Mr. Darcy, the flawed, suffering man who makes mistake after mistake through the book is
a classical Tragic Hero. After blundering through a one-sided romance between him and
Elizabeth, he attempts to propose to her, and is shot down horribly, saying that "You are
mistaken, Mr. Darcy, if you suppose that the mode of your declaration affected me in any other
way, than as it spared me the concern which I might have felt in refusing you, had you behaved in
a more gentlemanlike manner[Austen 109].” , showing just how much of his horrid manner has
obscured his good intentions. This Tragic Hero, with his pure heart and kind soul, has his actions
misunderstood and motives misinterpreted because of his demeanor. Truly, a Tragic Hero. But,
with every hero, there must be a Villain.

Mr. Wickham is the Villain of the book, and a despicable scoundrel to boot. He is
ludicrously self-serving, hurts many people and many families in pursuit of easy money and
easy girls, and has deceived both elizabeth and Mr. Darcy before. After running off with
her little sister, Elizabeth is beside herself, and laments that “"When I consider," she added in a yet
more agitated voice, "that I might have prevented it! I, who knew what he was. Had I but
explained some part of it only--some part of what I learnt, to my own family! Had his character
been known, this could not have happened. But it is all--all too late now[50]." Mr. Wickham had
deceived them all, all for his own selfish purpose. He has victimized many just to further his own
goals, including a fourteen year old Free Spirit named Lydia Bennett.

Lydia Bennet is the Youngest of the Bennett sisters, and a dictionary definition of the Free
Spirit. In ceaselessly pursuing men for such a young age she shows a great disregard for
danger, in caring for nothing but her own pleasures and feelings she is very hedonistic, and her
elopement with Wickham shows extreme irresponsibility of the highest degree. Her older sister
Elizabeth looks upon the sorry, sad state of her sister and her marriage, she thinks of “ how little of
permanent happiness could belong to a couple who were only brought together because their
passions were stronger than their virtue[170].” Led only by her wild feelings and flitting desires,
she leads herself to ruin, and her entire family to shame.

In literature, different characters are bound to be similar as the numerous stories are
created over the years. Such character tropes, while prevalent, do not lessen the creative worth of
the work they are found in. In Jane Austen’s Pride and Prejudice, there are several classical
archetypes, such as the Tragic Hero, the Villain, the Shrew, and the Free Spirit. These classical
characters, named Darcy, Wickham, Elizabeth, and Lydia perfectly match their archetype, but as
they interact with each other and unique social issues for Jane Austen’s time period, they are just
as interesting, and just as relevant to today’s society. People still make mistakes with the best
intentions. People still can become ill with greed and do horrible things. People still stand up to
authority figures and ignore all proper responsibility. And people still read Jane Austen’s Pride and
Prejudice, to learn about these human, relatable characters.
